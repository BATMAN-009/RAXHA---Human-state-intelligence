# Chapter 1.3–1.4 — The Magnetometer, the IMU, and Calibration

> **Why this chapter is third.** You now own two sensors: the accelerometer (which way is down, how hard was the hit) and the gyroscope (how fast am I turning, for seconds at a time). This chapter adds the third — the magnetometer (which way is north) — and then answers the question the first two chapters kept deferring: how do three imperfect sensors become one trustworthy estimate of *orientation*? The answer is the IMU as a system, and calibration as a lifelong discipline.

---

## 1. Definition

- A **magnetometer** measures the local magnetic field vector (µT). Earth's field (25–65 µT depending on latitude) gives an absolute heading reference — the only *absolute yaw* reference available in a wearable.
- An **IMU** (Inertial Measurement Unit) is the packaged combination: **6-axis** = 3-axis accelerometer + 3-axis gyroscope; **9-axis** (also "MARG") adds the 3-axis magnetometer.
- **Calibration** is the estimation and removal of each sensor's systematic errors (bias, scale, misalignment, magnetic distortion) — at the factory, at mount time, and continuously in the field.

**The core problem this chapter solves:** each sensor alone gives broken orientation. Accelerometer: roll/pitch when still, garbage during motion, and *no yaw at all* (rotating about gravity doesn't change gravity). Gyroscope: beautiful short-term rotation, unbounded drift (1.2 §3.1). Magnetometer: absolute yaw, but lies indoors. Orientation good enough to trust comes only from their marriage.

---

## 2. History (compressed)

Compass: ~11th century navigation. Fluxgate magnetometers: 1930s (aircraft, submarines). Solid-state era: **Hall-effect** and **AMR** (anisotropic magnetoresistance — Honeywell's thin-film sensors, 1990s) made compasses chip-sized; **TMR** (tunneling magnetoresistance) is the modern high-sensitivity option. **iPhone 3GS (2009)** put a magnetometer in every pocket (turn-by-turn navigation needed heading). InvenSense **MPU-9150 (2012)** packaged 9 axes in one part; today the magnetometer is often a separate die (AKM is the dominant supplier ✅) because magnetic sensing dislikes the gyro's hot, noisy neighborhood. Strapdown inertial navigation theory — the math of integrating body-frame sensors into world-frame orientation — comes from 1950s–60s aerospace (missiles and Apollo predate your watch by 60 years; the equations are identical, the budget is not).

## 3. Scientific Foundation

### 3.1 Earth's field and its two angles
The geomagnetic field points north *and down* (in the northern hemisphere): **declination** (angle between magnetic and true north — location-dependent, corrected from models like WMM) and **inclination** (dip angle — ~60–70° downward at mid-northern latitudes). Consequence: the horizontal component that actually gives heading is only ~½ or less of the total field — heading is a *projection*, and projecting requires knowing which way is down. **Magnetometer heading therefore depends on accelerometer tilt** — the sensors are entangled before fusion even begins.

### 3.2 Hard iron and soft iron — the two lies
- **Hard-iron distortion:** permanently magnetized material *on the device itself* (speaker magnets, motor, magnetic watch-band clasp) adds a **constant offset vector** in the sensor frame. Signature: the measured field traces a sphere whose *center is displaced* from the origin as the device rotates.
- **Soft-iron distortion:** ferromagnetic material that doesn't add field but *bends* it (steel frames, shields). Signature: the sphere becomes an **ellipsoid**.
- **Calibration = ellipsoid fitting:** collect field samples across many orientations (the "figure-8 dance" is user-facing choreography for exactly this), fit an ellipsoid, and solve for the offset (hard iron) and the 3×3 correction matrix (soft iron) that map it back to a centered sphere.
- **The uncorrectable lie:** *environmental* magnetism — rebar in floors, car bodies, elevators, laptops. It isn't attached to the device, so no self-calibration removes it. Indoors, heading errors of tens of degrees are routine. **Doctrine-level consequence: the magnetometer is a witness you consult, never one you trust unsupervised.**

### 3.3 The 6-vs-9-axis decision
- **6-axis fusion** (accel + gyro): absolute roll/pitch (gravity), *relative* yaw (gyro integration) — yaw drifts slowly but is *smooth and immune to magnetic lies*.
- **9-axis fusion:** magnetometer anchors yaw absolutely — but every indoor magnetic anomaly now tugs your orientation estimate.
- Modern practice (✅ visible in both Android's `GAME_ROTATION_VECTOR` — deliberately mag-free — and iOS's selectable attitude reference frames): **use 6-axis as the spine; admit the magnetometer only when its testimony is plausible** — field magnitude near Earth-nominal (25–65 µT) and inclination angle consistent with the local model. Implausible field → freeze the yaw correction, coast on the gyro.

### 3.4 Calibration as an error-budget discipline
Full error model per sensor: bias + scale factor + non-orthogonality (axes not perfectly 90°) + cross-sensor misalignment (accel triad vs gyro triad vs mag triad vs *the watch case itself*) + temperature coefficients + aging. Factory calibration handles nominal values; mounting stress and temperature shift them; field calibration never ends:
- **Accelerometer:** six-position method (factory); in-field, harvest rest periods (variance ≈ 0, |a| ≈ 1 g) to re-estimate bias.
- **Gyroscope:** bias = mean output during accel-confirmed stationarity; re-estimate continuously, index by temperature.
- **Magnetometer:** ellipsoid fit, refreshed opportunistically from natural wrist motion (users will not dance figure-8s for a safety product — auto-calibration is a product requirement, not a nicety).

## 4–5. Internal Working & Hardware (what matters for us)

Hall sensors measure the Lorentz-force voltage across a current-carrying plate; AMR/TMR measure resistance changes in magneto-sensitive films (better sensitivity/noise; AMR needs periodic "set/reset" degauss pulses to clear its own magnetization — visible in datasheets as flip/reset current). Consumer parts: ~0.1–0.6 µT noise, 10–100 Hz typical ODR, tens of µA — power is a non-issue compared to the gyro. Placement is the hard part: PCB layout must keep the magnetometer centimeters from speakers, haptics, and charging coils — and RAXHA has zero influence over this because *we don't build the watch*; we inherit whatever calibration quality the OEM shipped. 9-axis IMU packages exist, but flagship phones/watches often place a separate magnetometer away from the IMU for exactly this reason. **Watch-specific hazard (✅ common in the field): magnetic watch bands and clasps sit *on the device* — a hard-iron offset that changes every time the user swaps bands, invalidating prior calibration.**

## 6. Software

- **Android:** `TYPE_MAGNETIC_FIELD` (calibrated) / `_UNCALIBRATED` (raw + bias estimate); fused orientation via `TYPE_ROTATION_VECTOR` (9-axis), **`TYPE_GAME_ROTATION_VECTOR` (6-axis, magnetometer-free — note that Android ships both is itself the lesson)**, `TYPE_GEOMAGNETIC_ROTATION_VECTOR` (accel+mag, gyro-free, low power). Accuracy flags (`SENSOR_STATUS_ACCURACY_*`) report calibration confidence — read them; they are the OS telling you the compass is currently lying.
- **iOS/watchOS:** `CMDeviceMotion.attitude` with selectable reference frames: `.xArbitraryZVertical` (6-axis), `.xArbitraryCorrectedZVertical` (mag-assisted), `.xMagneticNorthZVertical` / `.xTrueNorthZVertical` (full compass, true north needs location for declination). `CLLocationManager.heading` for compass-style heading with its own accuracy field.
- Both platforms run continuous background magnetometer auto-calibration; you benefit from it and cannot control it — another reason to treat heading as advisory.

## 7. Data & 8. Algorithms

Practical fusion preview (full treatment in Module 8): the **complementary filter** — roll/pitch = low-passed gravity estimate + high-passed gyro integration; yaw = gyro integration, slowly corrected toward magnetometer heading *only when the field is plausible*. Madgwick/Mahony filters (6- or 9-axis) are the standard efficient implementations; a Kalman filter makes the same marriage with explicit uncertainty. **The design pattern to internalize — gated absolute references:** *drifting-but-smooth relative sensor as the spine; absolute-but-occasionally-lying sensor admitted only under plausibility checks.* You will meet this exact pattern again with GPS (absolute position that lies in urban canyons) and PPG-derived heart rate (absolute-ish physiology that lies under motion). Learn it once here; reuse it forever.

## 9. Real Products & 10. Research

✅ Every smartwatch/phone ships 6-axis IMU + (usually) magnetometer; compass apps, map orientation, and workout swim/rowing metrics consume it. Apple/Google publish nothing about their fusion internals (🔴); the *visible API design* — mag-free rotation vectors existing at all — is the strongest public evidence of magnetometer distrust (🟡 well-supported). Research anchors: **Madgwick 2010/2011** (open-source IMU/MARG orientation filter — among the most-used algorithms in wearable research; read it in Module 8), strapdown INS literature (Titterton & Weston's textbook is the canon), and indoor-heading correction research (map-matching, magnetic fingerprinting — an entire indoor-positioning subfield treats the *distortions themselves* as signal, which is a beautiful inversion worth remembering for Chapter 1.7).

## 11–12. Security & Medical (brief, honest)

Security: magnetometers can sense nearby electromagnetic activity (research demonstrations of covert channels and device-activity inference exist — lower practical severity than the accel/gyro channels; same mitigation posture). A *deliberate* strong magnet near the watch can corrupt heading and, on some devices, trigger magnetic-accessory detection — cross-sensor plausibility checks again. Medical: the magnetometer has essentially no direct physiological role; its clinical value is indirect — orientation quality feeds gait metrics, and heading feeds wandering detection (dementia elopement — a real and underserved clinical use case where "which direction and how far" matters).

## 13. Engineering for RAXHA

1. **Fall pipeline: 6-axis only.** Orientation change over a 2-second event window needs gyro + gravity; the magnetometer adds nothing but indoor lies. Explicitly exclude it from the Tier-1 confirmer's inputs.
2. **Heading is a Tier-2 context feature:** "moving away from home, heading roughly toward the main road" enriches a wandering-elder or walking-alone alert — consume platform-fused heading with its accuracy flag, display it as approximate, never route on it indoors.
3. **Calibration health is telemetry:** log platform calibration-accuracy flags and rest-period bias estimates per device. A watch whose accel bias drifted 100 mg or whose mag accuracy is pinned at "unreliable" is a degraded sensor — coverage telemetry (B1 §7) should know.
4. **Band-swap detection:** a sudden persistent shift in magnetic offset = probable band/accessory change → trigger recalibration, don't trust heading until it settles.

## 14. Failure Analysis

| Failure | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Heading 40° wrong indoors | Environmental soft/hard iron (rebar, desks) | Plausibility gating; treat heading as advisory; never in the fall path |
| Compass wrong after band swap | New hard-iron source on-device | Offset-shift detection → recalibrate |
| Orientation glitch near chargers/laptops | Transient field spikes | Field-magnitude gate; coast on 6-axis |
| Slow yaw rotation while user sits still | Gyro bias uncorrected (mag was gated out for hours) | Bounded coast time; re-admit mag on strict plausibility; accept yaw ambiguity honestly |
| Fusion confidently wrong after temperature swing | All three sensors' biases moved together | Temperature-indexed calibration; rest-period re-estimation |

## 15. RAXHA Application — distilled

Yaw is the one axis of orientation you can never fully trust on a wearable; design every feature so that yaw error degrades it gracefully rather than breaking it. Falls: yaw-free by construction. Wandering/heading context: advisory, plausibility-gated, uncertainty shown to responders ("heading approximately north" — never a confident arrow that's wrong).

## 16. Future

Magnetometer-free absolute yaw (visual/UWB anchors), magnetic-anomaly *positioning* (fingerprinting the distortions), self-calibrating IMU arrays, and factory-fused multi-die IMUs with per-unit thermal models shipped in firmware.

## 18. Founder Intelligence

**Why hasn't Apple solved this perfectly?** Indoor magnetic distortion is environmental — Apple can't calibrate away *your office's rebar*. Their visible answer (🟡 from API design): distrust by default, gate, and lean on 6-axis. The unsolved absolute-yaw-indoors problem is why UWB (Chapter 1.7) exists in their lineup at all. **WHOOP:** no display, no navigation use case → magnetometer essentially valueless to their business; sensor selection follows business model. **Garmin:** the opposite — navigation *is* the business (hiking, golf, aviation), so they ship ABC (altimeter-barometer-compass) sensors prominently and invest in calibration UX. **Google:** ships the distrust in the API itself (mag-free vectors, accuracy flags) — platform honesty as strategy. **Samsung:** hardware breadth; same fundamentals. **Why doesn't everyone fuse 9-axis?** Because worse-with-more-sensors is a real outcome — a lesson generalizable to RAXHA's whole multi-sensor thesis: *more evidence helps only when each witness is admitted under cross-examination.* **Startup opportunities:** indoor wayfinding for care facilities, magnetic-anomaly indoor positioning, dementia-elopement heading analytics, calibration-as-a-service for wearable OEMs. **RAXHA strategy:** spend zero differentiation effort on the magnetometer; consume platform fusion; invest the saved effort in the plausibility-gating pattern, which *is* differentiating and reappears in GPS and PPG. **PhD gaps:** lifelong self-calibration without user rituals; joint device+environment magnetic modeling; uncertainty-aware heading for emergency response. **Patents (Rigor Rule):** ellipsoid-fit auto-calibration families (assignees: Apple, Google, InvenSense/TDK, AKM — query `magnetometer calibration ellipsoid assignee:X`); magnetic-anomaly indoor positioning families (query `indoor positioning magnetic fingerprint`). **Ledger:** ✅ APIs, sensor suppliers, WMM; 🟡 fusion internals inferred from API design; 🔴 vendor calibration algorithms.

## 19. Design Review (highlights)

- **Garmin engineer:** "You dismiss the compass because you're wrist-bound indoors. Fine for falls — but your wandering feature will meet forests and trailheads where heading is *good* and GPS is degraded. Don't design distrust so deep you can't use the sensor where it shines."
- **Physician:** "Dementia elopement is a real clinical need and families will pay — but a wrong heading arrow sends a search party the wrong direction. Show uncertainty or show nothing."
- **Security researcher:** "An attacker with a magnet can close your plausibility gate at will — degrading you to drifting 6-axis yaw. Confirm nothing safety-critical depends on yaw. " *(It doesn't — §15. Say so in the threat model.)*
- **Investor:** "This chapter spends a week on a sensor you then decide not to differentiate on. Why?" *(Answer: the deliverable wasn't the sensor — it was the gated-absolute-reference pattern and the discipline to spend zero effort where there's no moat. Knowing where not to invest is the cheapest strategy work there is.)*

## 21. Chief Scientist's Verdict

**Confidence Ledger:** Ellipsoid-fit calibration corrects on-device hard/soft iron — ★★★★★ (textbook + universal deployment). Environmental distortion makes indoor heading unreliable — ★★★★★. Plausibility-gated 9-axis beats naive 9-axis — ★★★★☆ (strong practice + literature; "beats" magnitude varies by environment). Magnetic fingerprinting for indoor positioning — ★★★☆☆ (active research field, some deployments). Magnetometer value to fall detection — ★☆☆☆☆ (essentially none; excluded by design).
**TRL:** Consumer magnetometer — 9. Platform 6/9-axis fusion — 9. Auto-calibration without user ritual — 8–9 (shipped, imperfect). Magnetic indoor positioning — 4–6. RAXHA band-swap detection — 3 (our own idea; needs building).
**Roadmap:** *MVP:* consume platform fusion + accuracy flags; 6-axis fall path. *V2:* heading as gated context in wandering/walking-alone alerts; calibration-health telemetry. *Research:* magnetic-anomaly context ("user is in a vehicle/elevator"). *Never Build:* our own low-level fusion stack replacing the platform's (no moat, high maintenance); any safety decision that requires yaw.
**Competitor failures (sourced):** early smartphone navigation's compass complaints (documented user-facing "wave your phone in a figure 8" friction — calibration UX as a support burden); consumer drones' documented compass-interference flyaways near rebar/steel — the canonical "trusted the magnetometer, lost the vehicle" failure class RAXHA's gating pattern descends from.
**Kill Criteria:** If heading uncertainty can't be communicated to caregivers without confusing them (pilot usability testing), ship position-only and kill the heading display. If calibration-health telemetry shows >20% of fleet devices with chronically unreliable mag, deprecate heading features rather than degrade trust.
**Historical Failures (Historian):** Drone flyaways (above) — technology fine, trust model wrong. Segway — over-trusted sensor fusion marketing ("it can't fall") meets real-world edge cases; never promise what your error budget can't sign. Apple Maps launch (2012) — not a sensor failure but the canonical *shipped-before-validated* navigation failure; the lesson for any location/heading feature: wrongness in navigation is instantly visible, screenshot-able, and brand-defining.
