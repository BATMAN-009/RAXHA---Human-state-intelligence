# Chapter 1.1 — The MEMS Accelerometer

> **Why this chapter is first.** Every automatic emergency-detection feature that exists in a shipping consumer product today — Apple Watch fall detection, iPhone/Watch crash detection, Garmin incident detection — is built *on top of the accelerometer*. If RAXHA detects one thing reliably, it will be because you understand this sensor down to its physics, its noise floor, and its failure modes.

---

## 1. Definition

An **accelerometer** is a sensor that measures **proper acceleration** (also called *specific force*): the acceleration of the device relative to free fall, expressed in m/s² or in units of *g* (1 g = 9.80665 m/s²).

This definition contains the single most important — and most misunderstood — fact about the sensor:

- An accelerometer sitting motionless on a table reads **+1 g** (pointing up), not zero. The table is pushing it up, accelerating it away from the free-fall trajectory it would otherwise follow.
- An accelerometer in **free fall reads 0 g** on all axes, even though it is accelerating at 9.8 m/s² toward the ground.
- It therefore measures $\vec{f} = \vec{a} - \vec{g}$ : coordinate acceleration minus gravitational acceleration.

**Why it exists / what problem it solved.** Engineers needed to quantify vibration, shock, and motion in machines, vehicles, and aircraft — things you cannot measure with position sensors when there is no fixed reference nearby. An accelerometer is *self-contained*: it needs no external reference (no satellite, no beacon, no camera). That self-containment is why it is the backbone of inertial navigation, airbag triggering, and — for us — human motion sensing.

**MEMS** = Micro-Electro-Mechanical System: a mechanical structure (springs, a moving mass) etched into silicon at micrometer scale using the same fabrication processes as integrated circuits, co-packaged with the electronics that read it. MEMS is what made accelerometers cost $0.30 instead of $300, which is what put one in every phone and watch on Earth.

## 3. Scientific Foundation

### 3.1 Physics: why "proper acceleration"

By Einstein's **equivalence principle**, no local experiment can distinguish uniform gravity from uniform acceleration. An accelerometer *is* that local experiment — so it fundamentally cannot separate the two. It reports specific force:

$$\vec{f}_{measured} = \vec{a}_{device} - \vec{g}$$

Consequences you will use constantly:

1. **Orientation sensing:** at rest, the measurement *is* the gravity vector in the device frame → tilt/orientation (this is how your phone rotates its screen, and how RAXHA can tell "person upright" from "person lying down").
2. **Free-fall detection:** total magnitude near 0 g means the device is falling — the opening signature of many fall-detection algorithms.
3. **You cannot get linear acceleration without removing gravity**, which requires knowing orientation, which requires fusion with other sensors or filtering assumptions. (Chapter 1.4 / Module 8.)

### 3.2 Mechanics: the mass-spring-damper

Every accelerometer, from 1920 to today, is the same machine: a **proof mass** $m$ suspended by a **spring** $k$, with **damping** $b$, inside a case attached to the thing being measured. When the case accelerates, the mass lags behind; the spring deflection $x$ encodes the acceleration.

Newton's second law in the case's reference frame:

$$m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = -m a_{case}$$

This is a second-order system with:

- **Natural (resonant) frequency:** $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$
- **Quality factor:** $Q = \sqrt{km}/b$ (how underdamped it is)
- **Static sensitivity:** for slowly varying acceleration ($\omega \ll \omega_0$), $x = -\frac{m}{k}a = -\frac{a}{\omega_0^2}$

**The fundamental design trade-off falls straight out of that last equation:** displacement per g scales as $1/\omega_0^2$. A *soft* spring / heavy mass gives big, easy-to-measure displacement (high sensitivity) but low bandwidth and fragility; a *stiff* spring gives wide bandwidth and shock survival but tiny displacement. Consumer parts pick $\omega_0/2\pi$ of a few kHz, giving proof-mass displacements of **sub-nanometer per milli-g** — you are measuring motions smaller than an atom's diameter.

### 3.3 Noise: the thermodynamic floor

The damper is a dissipative element, so by the **fluctuation–dissipation theorem** it injects a random Brownian force with power spectral density $S_F = 4k_BTb$ (same physics as Johnson noise in a resistor). Dividing by mass, the **noise-equivalent acceleration density** is:

$$a_n = \frac{\sqrt{4k_BTb}}{m} = \sqrt{\frac{4k_BT\,\omega_0}{mQ}} \quad \left[\mathrm{\frac{m/s^2}{\sqrt{Hz}}}\right]$$

Read this equation the way a sensor designer does:

- **Bigger proof mass → lower noise.** This is *the* reason a $200 tactical-grade sensor outperforms a $0.30 consumer part: it's physically larger.
- Higher Q (lower damping, often via a low-pressure sealed cavity) lowers noise but causes ringing at resonance.
- Typical consumer wearable parts: **25–180 µg/√Hz**. At 50 Hz bandwidth that's roughly 0.2–1.3 milli-g RMS — far below the multi-g signatures of impacts, but relevant when you try to detect subtle things (respiration from chest motion, tremor).

### 3.4 Transduction: capacitance

Modern consumer MEMS accelerometers are almost universally **capacitive**. A parallel-plate capacitor has $C = \varepsilon A/d$. Attach one plate to the proof mass and one to the frame; motion changes $d$, changing $C$. To get linearity and reject common-mode effects (temperature, supply), the proof mass sits **between two fixed electrodes** forming a *differential* pair:

$$\Delta C = C_1 - C_2 \approx \frac{2C_0}{d_0}x \quad \text{(for } x \ll d_0\text{)}$$

The full signal chain therefore transforms: **acceleration → force → displacement → capacitance change → voltage → digital number.** Typical capacitance changes are **attofarads to femtofarads** ($10^{-18}$–$10^{-15}$ F) — smaller than the parasitic capacitance of the bond wires — which is why the readout circuit must sit on-die or millimeter-close, and why the ADXL50's monolithic integration was such a breakthrough.

Other transduction principles you should recognize: **piezoresistive** (strain changes resistance; simple, temperature-sensitive; used in high-g shock sensors), **piezoelectric** (charge from stress; AC-only; vibration test equipment), **thermal convection** (no moving parts; slow), and **resonant/frequency-modulated** (acceleration shifts a resonator's frequency; emerging in high-precision parts).

## 4. Internal Working — the complete signal path

1. **MEMS structure.** A polysilicon proof mass (tens of micrograms) suspended by folded-beam springs, with dozens of interdigitated **comb fingers** forming the differential capacitors. Three separate structures (or one cleverly-shared one) sense X, Y, Z; the Z axis typically uses a "teeter-totter" torsional plate with asymmetric mass.
2. **Excitation & C/V conversion.** A high-frequency carrier (hundreds of kHz — well above mechanical response) is driven onto the fixed plates; a **switched-capacitor charge amplifier** converts ΔC into voltage. Modulating above baseband moves the signal away from 1/f electronic noise (chopper stabilization / correlated double sampling).
3. **Demodulation & anti-alias filtering.**
4. **ADC.** Usually a **sigma-delta (ΣΔ)** converter — a natural fit because it trades sampling speed for resolution and its loop filter doubles as noise shaping.
5. **Digital engine.** Decimation filters set the **output data rate (ODR)**; a register map exposes range (±2/4/8/16 g), ODR, filter bandwidth; an **interrupt engine** implements in-hardware detectors (free-fall, high-g, tap/double-tap, activity/inactivity, orientation); a **FIFO** (hundreds to thousands of samples) buffers data so the host CPU can sleep.
6. **(High-end only) Closed-loop force feedback:** instead of letting the mass move, an electrostatic force servo holds it centered and the feedback effort *is* the output. Gives superb linearity and dynamic range; used in seismic/navigation-grade, not in wearables.

**Fabrication essentials:** surface micromachining deposits a structural polysilicon layer over a sacrificial oxide; etching the oxide away ("release") frees the moving parts. The classic release-stage failure is **stiction** (surface forces gluing the mass down), mitigated with anti-stiction coatings. The MEMS die is capped at wafer level to seal a cavity at a controlled pressure — the pressure sets the damping $b$, hence Q, hence the noise/ringing balance. The MEMS die and the CMOS ASIC are then stacked or placed side-by-side in a ~2×2 mm LGA package.

## 5. Hardware — what you actually buy and integrate

Representative parts (all publicly documented datasheet values):

| Part | Type | Noise | Current | Notable |
|------|------|-------|---------|---------|
| ST **LSM6DSO/DSV** | 6-axis IMU | ~70 µg/√Hz | ~0.55 mA high-perf; µA in low-power | The archetypal phone/watch IMU; FIFO, ML core on newer variants |
| Bosch **BMA400** | 3-axis accel | ~180 µg/√Hz | **14.5 µA** always-on | Designed for wearable always-on step/activity |
| Analog Devices **ADXL362** | 3-axis accel | 175–375 µg/√Hz | **1.8 µA @ 100 Hz**, 270 nA wake-on-motion | The ultra-low-power reference point |
| Bosch **BHI360** class | Smart sensor hub | — | µA-scale | Runs sensor-fusion/HAR firmware *inside the sensor* |

Integration realities:

- **Range vs resolution:** a 16-bit output over ±2 g resolves ~61 µg/LSB; over ±16 g, ~488 µg/LSB. Fall impacts commonly exceed ±16 g at the wrist — a standard consumer part **clips (saturates)** during the most important milliseconds. Apple's answer was a dedicated high-g (up to 256 g) accelerometer alongside the low-g one.
- **Power hierarchy:** the sensor itself is nearly free (µA). The expensive things are (a) waking the application processor to look at data and (b) radioing raw data off the device. Hence: use the sensor's interrupt engine and FIFO; process locally; transmit *decisions*, not waveforms.
- **Mounting matters:** PCB stress, adhesive cure, and reflow soldering shift the zero-g offset (typically ±20–50 mg tolerance in datasheets, plus temperature drift ~±0.1–1 mg/°C).

## 6. Software — OS APIs you will build on

### Android / Wear OS
- `SensorManager` with `Sensor.TYPE_ACCELEROMETER` (raw, includes gravity, m/s²), `TYPE_LINEAR_ACCELERATION` and `TYPE_GRAVITY` (fusion-derived), `TYPE_SIGNIFICANT_MOTION`, `TYPE_STEP_DETECTOR` (hardware-offloaded).
- **Batching:** `registerListener(..., maxReportLatencyUs)` lets the sensor hub buffer samples in its FIFO and deliver them in bursts while the AP sleeps — the single most important API for battery-viable continuous sensing.
- **Wake-up vs non-wake-up sensors:** non-wake-up sensors silently drop or buffer data while the SoC sleeps; for safety-critical detection you must use wake-up variants or hub-offloaded triggers.
- Since Android 12, sampling **above 200 Hz** requires the `HIGH_SAMPLING_RATE_SENSORS` permission (a side-channel mitigation — see §11).
- Wear OS adds **Health Services**, which offloads activity/exercise detection to the low-power hub.

### iOS / watchOS
- **Core Motion:** `CMMotionManager` (raw accelerometer, ~100 Hz max), `CMDeviceMotion` (fusion output with `userAcceleration` and `gravity` conveniently separated; units of g).
- **`CMSensorRecorder`** (watchOS): records accelerometer at 50 Hz for later batch retrieval — lets you capture data without keeping your app alive.
- **`CMFallDetectionManager`** (watchOS 7+, entitlement required): delivers Apple's own fall events to third-party apps — RAXHA on Apple hardware would *consume* this rather than reimplement it, because Apple does not let third-party apps run truly continuous high-rate background sensing on watchOS.
- Raw accelerometer data is **not** in HealthKit; HealthKit exposes derived quantities (steps, workouts, fall events count).

**Platform-strategy takeaway (important for RAXHA):** on Apple hardware you build *on top of* Apple's detectors; on Wear OS/Android you can run your own models but must engineer aggressively around Doze, background limits, and OEM battery killers. This asymmetry will shape the whole product architecture.

## 7. Data

- **Units:** Android gives m/s²; iOS gives g. Normalize immediately at ingestion; unit confusion is a classic integration bug.
- **Sampling rate:** voluntary human movement lives below ~20 Hz, so 50 Hz captures gait/activity comfortably (Nyquist). **Impacts are broadband** — fall/crash spikes are milliseconds long — so detection-grade capture wants 100–200 Hz+ (Apple's crash pipeline samples the high-g part in the kHz region).
- **Gravity separation:** low-pass at ~0.3 Hz ≈ gravity estimate; subtract for linear acceleration; or take the fused `userAcceleration`/`TYPE_LINEAR_ACCELERATION`. Caveat: during sustained dynamics (a car turning, a fall's rotation) the low-pass assumption breaks precisely when you care most.
- **Error model:** each axis suffers **bias** (zero-g offset, drifts with temperature), **scale-factor error**, **axis misalignment/cross-axis sensitivity** (~1–2%), and noise. The standard field calibration is the **six-position method** (each axis up/down against known ±1 g) solving for bias and scale; for RAXHA, per-device auto-calibration from rest periods matters because you'll never get users to do calibration rituals.
- **Timestamps:** sensors timestamp in their own clock domain; expect jitter and occasional dropped samples; resample onto a uniform grid before feature extraction. When fusing accel with PPG/GPS across chips, clock alignment is a real engineering task.
- **Volume:** 3 axes × 16 bit × 100 Hz ≈ 600 B/s ≈ 52 MB/day/device raw. Fine on-device, wasteful to ship to cloud — a privacy *and* power argument for edge processing.

## 8. Algorithms

**The base signal:** magnitude $SMV = \sqrt{a_x^2+a_y^2+a_z^2}$ — orientation-invariant, so it's the workhorse for detection independent of how the watch sits on the wrist.

### Classical threshold fall detection (Bourke et al., 2007 lineage)
A fall has a canonical four-phase accelerometer signature:
1. **Pre-fall / free-fall phase:** SMV drops toward 0 g (body in ballistic descent) — brief, sometimes absent in slow collapses.
2. **Impact:** SMV spike, typically > 2–3 g at the waist, often far higher (and clipped) at the wrist.
3. **Posture change:** gravity vector in device frame differs from pre-fall orientation (upright → lying).
4. **Post-impact inactivity:** low signal variance for tens of seconds.

Threshold algorithms AND these conditions together. They are cheap enough to run in the sensor's interrupt engine, but suffer high false-positive rates (sitting down hard, tossing the watch on a bed) and miss soft falls. **Modern practice: thresholds as a wake-up trigger, ML as the confirmer.**

### Feature-based ML
Window the signal (2–5 s, 50% overlap); per window compute time-domain features (mean, variance, signal magnitude area, zero crossings, inter-axis correlation) and frequency-domain features (FFT band energies, spectral entropy, dominant frequency); classify with Random Forest / SVM. This was the state of the art from Bao & Intille (2004) until ~2016 and is *still* the right choice when you have little data and need interpretability.

### Deep learning
- **CNNs** learn the features from raw windows; **DeepConvLSTM** (Ordóñez & Roggen, 2016) added recurrence for temporal context and became the standard HAR baseline.
- **Transformers / self-supervised pretraining** (2019→): pretrain on massive unlabeled wearable data (e.g., multi-task SSL — Saeed et al. 2019; UK Biobank pretraining — Yuan et al. 2024), fine-tune on small labeled sets. This is the current research frontier and directly relevant to RAXHA's scarce-label problem (real falls are rare).
- **TinyML:** quantized (int8) CNNs of 10–100 kB running on a Cortex-M or on-sensor ML core, so the always-on tier costs microamps.

### The cascade pattern (memorize this — it recurs all through RAXHA)
> **Tier 0:** sensor interrupt engine (free-fall/high-g thresholds), ~µA, always on →
> **Tier 1:** TinyML window classifier on watch MCU, runs only when Tier 0 fires →
> **Tier 2:** full-context model on phone (adds location, activity history, heart rate) →
> **Tier 3:** human confirmation UI (countdown), then alert.
> Each tier is more accurate and more expensive; each only runs when the tier below suspects something.

## 10. Research — landmark papers

1. **Roylance & Angell, 1979**, "A batch-fabricated silicon accelerometer," *IEEE T-ED.* The founding MEMS accelerometer paper. Matters because it proved batch semiconductor fabrication of mechanical sensors — the cost curve that put sensors on every wrist.
2. **Bao & Intille, 2004**, "Activity recognition from user-annotated acceleration data," *Pervasive.* Established multi-site accelerometer HAR with classical ML; the citation ancestor of all wearable HAR.
3. **Bourke, O'Brien & Lyons, 2007**, "Evaluation of a threshold-based tri-axial accelerometer fall detection algorithm," *Gait & Posture.* Canonical threshold fall detection; defined the impact/posture test structure everyone benchmarks against.
4. **Ordóñez & Roggen, 2016**, "Deep convolutional and LSTM recurrent neural networks for multimodal wearable activity recognition," *Sensors.* DeepConvLSTM — the default deep HAR baseline.
5. **Dey et al., 2014**, "AccelPrint: Imperfections of accelerometers make smartphones trackable," *NDSS.* Manufacturing imperfections fingerprint individual chips → privacy implications (§11).
6. **Trippel et al., 2017**, "WALNUT: Waging doubt on the integrity of MEMS accelerometers with acoustic injection attacks," *IEEE EuroS&P.* Sound at the proof mass's resonant frequency can inject attacker-controlled false readings (§11).
7. **Datasets you must know:** *UCI-HAR / WISDM / PAMAP2* (activity recognition), *SisFall, MobiAct* (simulated falls, young subjects — mind the domain gap), **FARSEEING** (real-world falls of older adults captured in the wild — small but gold-standard; the gap between SisFall performance and FARSEEING performance is the central honesty check of the field).
8. **Frontier (2019→):** self-supervised pretraining for IMU (Saeed et al. 2019 multi-task SSL; Yuan et al. 2024 UK Biobank-scale pretraining, npj Digital Medicine); on-device/foundation-model approaches to motion understanding. **Open problems:** real-world (not lab) fall benchmarks, wrist-site low-impact fall detection, personalization without labels, and quantified uncertainty for alert decisions.

## 11. Security & Privacy

Threat models to internalize:

- **Side-channel inference:** accelerometers sense vibrations, so they partially overhear the world. Demonstrated attacks: keystroke/PIN inference from device motion (Owusu et al., *ACCessory*, 2012), inferring nearby keyboard typing (*(sp)iPhone*, Marquardt et al., CCS 2011), and speech recovery from ear-speaker vibrations (*Spearphone/AccelEve*, 2019–20). This is why Android 12+ gates >200 Hz sampling behind a permission and why "it's just motion data" is a false comfort.
- **Device fingerprinting:** *AccelPrint* (NDSS 2014) showed per-chip calibration imperfections (bias/scale) uniquely identify devices across apps/sites. RAXHA stores calibration data — treat it as an identifier.
- **Signal injection:** *WALNUT* (2017) acoustically forced false outputs; a hypothetical attacker could suppress or spoof a fall signature. Defenses: mechanical isolation, sampling-frequency randomization, cross-sensor consistency checks (a real fall also shows in gyro, barometer, PPG context).
- **Behavioral privacy:** continuous accelerometry reveals gait (a biometric), activity, sleep, even intoxication. RAXHA's own manifesto values imply: process on device, transmit events not waveforms, encrypt at rest, minimize retention. (Module 13 goes deep.)

## 12. Medical Perspective

- **The metrics that govern everything:** *sensitivity* (fraction of true falls detected) vs *specificity* (fraction of non-falls correctly ignored), and — critically for a rare event — **false alarms per unit time**. A detector with 99% specificity evaluated per-10-second-window still fires ~86 false alarms/day. For rare events, per-window specificity is a nearly meaningless number; report **false alarms per person-week**.
- **Alarm fatigue** is the clinically documented consequence: too many false alerts and family members (and users) start ignoring or disabling the system — the failure mode that kills the product's entire purpose.
- **Lab-to-life gap:** algorithms scoring >95% on simulated falls (young volunteers on mats) degrade sharply on real-world elderly falls (FARSEEING-type data), which are slower, softer, and preceded by abnormal movement (dizziness, syncope).
- **Regulatory position:** consumer fall detection (Apple, Samsung, Garmin) ships as a *safety/wellness feature*, not an FDA-cleared medical device (unlike Apple's ECG/AFib features, which went through FDA De Novo/clearance). RAXHA's likely path: launch as wellness/safety with carefully honest claims; pursue regulatory clearance only for specific medical claims (e.g., "detects falls in adults 65+" as a claim would attract scrutiny). *Never* market detection performance you have not measured on the population you claim. (Module 16 covers FDA/CE pathways in depth.)

## 13. Engineering — how RAXHA builds with this sensor

**Architecture (MVP-grade):**

1. **Watch tier:** subscribe to hardware wake-triggers (free-fall/high-g interrupt or platform fall event); keep a rolling raw buffer (e.g., 10 s pre / 10 s post trigger via FIFO or `CMSensorRecorder`); run a quantized TinyML confirmer on the buffer.
2. **Phone tier:** receives event + feature summary over BLE; fuses context (GPS speed, geofence, time-of-day, recent activity, HR if available); runs the escalation state machine.
3. **Escalation:** local alarm + countdown (30–60 s, like Apple) → user cancel → else notify trusted contacts with live location; contact acknowledgment loop; fallback to secondary contacts.
4. **Cloud tier:** delivery infrastructure, contact management, audit logs — *not* raw sensor storage.

**Battery math you must do:** always-on 100 Hz sensing with hub batching ≈ single-digit mA·h/day — acceptable; waking the AP for every threshold trip is what kills batteries, so Tier-1 must run on the MCU/hub. **Testing:** a physical drop/ADL (activities of daily living) test rig, replayable recorded datasets in CI, and a "shadow mode" deployment (log would-have-alerted decisions without alerting) before enabling real alerts.

**How the giants would each build it:** *Apple:* vertical — custom high-g sensor, on-device only, conservative defaults, feature not product. *Google:* platform — Health Services API + fleet learning (federated). *Medical-device co:* narrow claim, clinical trial, locked algorithm, FDA clearance, sells to health systems. *RAXHA the startup:* build on platform detectors where they exist (Apple), own the *response layer* (family alerting, context fusion, multi-signal escalation) — the layer none of the above actually owns.

## 14. Failure Analysis — when the accelerometer story breaks

| Failure | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| **Soft/slow falls missed** (the clinically common case: elderly person slides down a wall during syncope) | No free-fall phase, no impact spike — the canonical signature is absent | Posture + prolonged-inactivity + physiological context detection, not impact detection |
| **Wrist-site clipping** | Impacts at the wrist exceed ±16 g; peak information lost | High-g range part, or classify on clip-robust features |
| **Loose strap / off-wrist** | Signal decoupled from the body; watch on a nightstand "falls" | On-body detection (PPG proximity), strap-fit inference |
| **Vigorous-activity false positives** | Volleyball, boxing, hammering, roller coasters mimic impacts | Context gating (activity recognition, location, repetition patterns) — exactly Apple's roller-coaster lesson |
| **Vehicle vibration** | Sustained broadband vibration confuses activity models | Driving detection (GPS speed + spectral signature) as an explicit state |
| **Temperature bias drift** | Zero-g offset moves mg-scale per °C; matters for posture estimation | Continuous auto-calibration during detected rest |
| **Sample drops under OS pressure** | Doze/App Standby/OEM killers throttle listeners | Wake-up sensors, hub offload, watchdog + data-gap telemetry |
| **The base-rate trap** | Even superb per-event specificity yields mostly-false alarms because real falls are rare | Multi-sensor confirmation, user cancel window, report FA/person-week honestly |

## 15. RAXHA Application — the distilled doctrine

- **Use it as:** the always-on, microamp-cheap *trigger layer* and the posture/activity/impact primary sensor. Nothing else in the stack is as power-proportionate.
- **Do NOT use it as:** a standalone verdict. An accelerometer event is a *hypothesis*; context (location, activity, physiology, time) turns it into a *decision*. Single-sensor fall detection at the wrist is a false-alarm machine.
- **MVP:** consume platform fall events (Apple) / own lightweight cascade (Wear OS) + best-in-class family escalation. **Production evolution:** own models trained on shadow-mode fleet data, personalized baselines, federated updates.

## 16. Future

- **On-sensor intelligence:** ML cores inside the accelerometer package running the entire Tier-0/1 cascade at µA.
- **Event-driven sampling:** sensors that adapt rate to signal activity (adaptive sampling / concept from neuromorphic event cameras migrating to inertial sensing).
- **Single-die wide dynamic range:** micro-g sensitivity *and* 100+ g survival/measurement in one part (Apple's two-sensor approach becoming one sensor).

- **Open problems worth a startup's attention:** real-world validated *soft-fall* detection; label-free personalization; calibrated uncertainty on alerts ("87% confident" changes the escalation policy).

## 18. Founder Intelligence

**Why hasn't Apple solved this perfectly?** Because the accelerometer's limit isn't engineering — it's *epistemology*. A perfect, noiseless, infinite-range accelerometer still (a) cannot distinguish gravity from acceleration (equivalence principle — no budget fixes physics), (b) sees only the wrist, not the body, and (c) measures *motion*, while the event RAXHA cares about is a *human state* (helpless, injured). That's why Apple needs gyro + barometer + GPS + mic + ML on top: the problem is inference from incomplete evidence, and it stays open forever. **Founder lesson: markets built on inference problems never close — there is always room to infer better.**

**Why is WHOOP different?** ✅ No screen, no apps, subscription business, 24/7 physiological focus (strain/recovery/sleep). Its accelerometer serves *behavior understanding*, not safety — WHOOP ships no fall detection at all. 🟡 Inference: deliberate scope discipline — safety features carry liability, emergency infrastructure, and support costs that don't fit a recovery-coaching subscription. **Lesson: what a company *refuses* to build tells you its real business model.**

**Why is Garmin different?** ✅ Battery-first culture (weeks, not days), owns its entire stack including the OS, incident detection scoped to GPS activities only. That scoping is a *strategic* choice: detection only where context priors are favorable = bounded false alarms + bounded battery cost, no 24/7 promise to defend. **Lesson: scoping the promise is a legitimate alternative to solving the general problem.**

**Why is Google different?** ✅ Platform play: Health Services, sensor API standardization, federated learning infrastructure. Google wins if *everyone* builds sensing apps on Android, so it commoditizes the layer Apple keeps proprietary. Pixel Watch fall detection arrived ~5 years after Apple's (2023). **Lesson: platforms move slower but define the field you play on.**

**Why is Samsung different?** ✅ Hardware breadth + fast-follower features + Knox security stack; fall detection exists with adjustable sensitivity. 🟡 Fragmentation across models/regions dilutes any single safety promise. **Lesson: a feature that exists is not a promise that's kept — RAXHA competes against the *promise*, not the feature list.**

**Why doesn't everyone do this?** Liability (a missed fall = lawsuit + headline), emergency-services integration is per-country regulatory plumbing, false alarms burn brand trust, validation on elderly populations is slow and expensive, and the support burden of a safety product (24/7 expectations) is a different company than an app company. These barriers are RAXHA's *moat if crossed* — hard things repel competitors exactly as much as they repel you.

**Startup opportunities adjacent to this chapter:** senior-care B2B (fall detection sold to care facilities/insurers, reimbursement-driven), lone-worker compliance (regulated industries must monitor isolated staff), construction/mining impact + man-down monitoring, insurance telemetry (activity-verified premiums), clinical-trial movement endpoints (pharma pays for objective mobility measures), pet/livestock variants. Note the pattern: same sensor, same algorithms — the company is defined by *who pays and why*.

**What would RAXHA do (strategy, not engineering)?** Sample accel continuously at 50–100 Hz (µA-cheap, the one sensor where "always" is affordable) — but the strategic decision is *where the moat goes*: not detection (platforms absorb it) but the **response layer** — family experience, acknowledgment loops, cross-platform coverage, trust. Closest strategic comparable: ✅ Life360 (family safety network, freemium subscription, ~$1B+ revenue scale) — proof that families pay for *peace of mind infrastructure*, not sensors. RAXHA = Life360's model with a medically serious detection layer.

**Research that doesn't exist yet (PhD-grade gaps = future moats):**

3. Calibrated uncertainty for escalation policy ("fire at 60% confidence if user is 80+ and alone" — decision-theoretic alerting).
4. A public, large, real-world wrist-worn fall benchmark — whoever builds it owns the field's yardstick.

## 21. Chief Scientist's Verdict

| Claim | Confidence | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Wrist accelerometers detect high-impact falls | ★★★★★ | Commercial products (Apple, Samsung), clinical studies, decades of literature |

| Threshold-only detection is production-adequate | ★★☆☆☆ | Contradicted by real-world false-alarm evidence — thresholds are triggers, not products |

**TRL** (scoped capabilities): MEMS accelerometer sensor — 9. Wrist impact-fall detection — 8–9 (shipping products). Wrist soft-fall detection — 3–4. Wrist fall-*risk* prediction — 4–5.

**Roadmap Placement:** *MVP:* impact-fall detection (platform events on iOS; own cascade on Android). *V2:* personalized thresholds + shadow-trained models. *Research Lab:* soft-fall detection (needs physiological fusion + evidence that doesn't exist yet). *Never Build (on accel alone):* syncope detection, "emotion from motion" (★, TRL 2–3 — building it would violate the evidence firewall).

**Competitor Failure Analysis (sourced):** Apple — documented false-positive waves (crash detection on roller coasters, 2022 press; fall alerts during vigorous sports), fall detection default-off under 55 (an honest admission that false alarms outweigh benefit for the young). Medical pendants — published real-world studies show high false-alarm burden and, critically, *non-wear*: the device fails because it stigmatizes (users don't wear it), a human-factors failure no algorithm fixes. Life360 — location-data-broker scandal (documented 2021–22): trust, once monetized, doesn't come back.

**Kill Criteria (pre-registered):** Shadow-mode false alarms >1 per user-week after context gating → do not enable alerts. Real-world-style validation sensitivity <60% for a claimed population → the claim doesn't ship. If platform detectors + RAXHA response layer outperform RAXHA's own Android model after two quarters → kill the own-model program and ride the platforms without ego.

**Historical Failures (Historian's ledger):**
- **First-generation fall pendants** — the science worked; the *wearing* failed. Stigma ("that's for old people") → non-wear → zero protection. Axis: user behavior. RAXHA's inoculation: the sensor is a normal watch people already want.

- **Basis** (Intel, recalled 2016) — watch recalled for overheating. Axis: hardware safety. Lesson: a safety brand dies fastest from a hardware safety failure.
- **Theranos** — governance, not technology: claims outran evidence with no internal audit function. Axis: scientific governance. The Confidence Ledger (§21.1) is this chapter's structural antibody.
